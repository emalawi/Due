Chapter 1: Theoretical Foundations of Metamorphic Proteins and Evolutionary Couplings

1.1 The Paradigm Shift: From Anfinsen's Dogma to Metamorphic Proteins

For over half a century, structural biology operated under Anfinsen’s thermodynamic hypothesis: the primary amino acid sequence of a protein dictates a single, unique, and stable three-dimensional tertiary structure at its global thermodynamic minimum. This "one sequence, one structure" paradigm served as the foundational pillar for structural biology and computational protein design.

However, the discovery of metamorphic proteins has fundamentally challenged this dogma. Metamorphic proteins are single polypeptide chains that reversibly transition between two or more topologically distinct folded states under physiological conditions. This conformational duality is not merely local flexibility or a transition between active and inactive states; it involves complete structural reorganizations, including the wholesale rewiring of secondary structure elements (e.g., transitioning from an $\alpha$-helix to a $\beta$-sheet) and the complete remodeling of the hydrophobic core.

                   Anfinsen's Single-State Dogma:
                    [Sequence] ---> [Folded State]
                    
              Metamorphic Multi-State Paradigm:
                                 /---> [Conformation A] (State A)
                    [Sequence] --|
                                 \---> [Conformation B] (State B)

Prominent biological examples demonstrate the functional necessity of metamorphic behavior:

  • Lymphotactin (XCL1): A chemokine that switches between a monomeric three-stranded $\beta$-sheet/$\alpha$-helix fold (which binds and activates the XCR1 G-protein coupled receptor) and a dimeric all-$\beta$ fold (which binds cell-surface glycosaminoglycans).
  • Mad2 (Mitotic Arrest Deficient 2): A vital spindle assembly checkpoint protein that alternates between open (O-Mad2) and closed (C-Mad2) topologies to regulate the cell division cycle.
  • KaiB: A cyanobacterial circadian clock protein that slowly transitions between an inactive ground state and an active fold, acting as a molecular hourglass.

Design of these systems requires an entirely different computational framework than classical de novo design. Instead of optimizing a sequence to fit a single target backbone, the designer must engineer a sequence with a multi-stable, highly flat energy landscape, where the free energy barrier ($\Delta G^\ddagger$) and the relative population occupancy of the states are precisely tuned.

1.2 Thermodynamics of Multi-State Energy Landscapes

The conformational behavior of metamorphic proteins is governed by statistical mechanics and the partition function of the polypeptide chain. Let a metamorphic protein sequence $S$ have access to two distinct folded structural ensembles, State $\mathcal{A}$ and State $\mathcal{B}$, alongside the unfolded state ensemble $\mathcal{U}$.

                      [Transition State ‡]
                             / \
                            /   \
              State A      /     \      State B
               /\_        /       \        _/\
              /   \______/         \______/   \
             /                                 \
            /                                   \
   ________/                                     \________
   Reaction Coordinate (e.g., Q_A - Q_B)

1.2.1 The Partition Function and State Populations

The total partition function $Z$ of the system is the sum of the microstate Boltzmann factors:

$$Z = Z_{\mathcal{U}} + Z_{\mathcal{A}} + Z_{\mathcal{B}} = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{U}} e^{-\beta E_i} + \sum_{j \in \mathcal{A}} e^{-\beta E_j} + \sum_{k \in \mathcal{B}} e^{-\beta E_k}$$

where $\beta = \frac{1}{k_B T}$, $k_B$ is the Boltzmann constant, and $T$ is the absolute temperature. The equilibrium free energy $G_{\mathcal{X}}$ of any macroscopic state $\mathcal{X} \in {\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B}}$ is defined as:

$$G_{\mathcal{X}} = -k_B T \ln Z_{\mathcal{X}} = -k_B T \ln \left( \sum_{m \in \mathcal{X}} e^{-E_m / k_B T} \right)$$

The probability $P(\mathcal{X})$ of the protein occupying state $\mathcal{X}$ is given by:

$$P(\mathcal{X}) = \frac{Z_{\mathcal{X}}}{Z} = \frac{e^{-G_{\mathcal{X}}/k_B T}}{e^{-G_{\mathcal{U}}/k_B T} + e^{-G_{\mathcal{A}}/k_B T} + e^{-G_{\mathcal{B}}/k_B T}}$$

For a metamorphic protein to be viable under physiological conditions, both states must be significantly populated, implying that the free energy difference $\Delta G_{\mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{B}}$ is highly constrained:

$$\Delta G_{\mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{B}} = G_{\mathcal{B}} - G_{\mathcal{A}} = -k_B T \ln \left( \frac{[\mathcal{B}]}{[\mathcal{A}]} \right)$$

Typically, $|\Delta G_{\mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{B}}| \le 2.0 \text{ kcal/mol}$. If $|\Delta G_{\mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{B}}| > 3.0 \text{ kcal/mol}$, the population of the minor state falls below $1%$, and the system effectively behaves as a static, single-state protein.

1.2.2 Kinetics of Interconversion

The rate of interconversion between State $\mathcal{A}$ and State $\mathcal{B}$ is governed by the height of the transition state barrier $\Delta G^\ddagger$. According to Transition State Theory, the forward and reverse rate constants ($k_{\mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{B}}$ and $k_{\mathcal{B} \to \mathcal{A}}$) are:

$$k_{\mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{B}} = \kappa \frac{k_B T}{h} e^{-\Delta G^\ddagger_{\mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{B}} / k_B T}$$

$$k_{\mathcal{B} \to \mathcal{A}} = \kappa \frac{k_B T}{h} e^{-\Delta G^\ddagger_{\mathcal{B} \to \mathcal{A}} / k_B T}$$

where $\kappa$ is the transmission coefficient and $h$ is Planck's constant. In metamorphic proteins, this barrier must be high enough to prevent spontaneous thermal fluctuations from averaging out the distinct structures into a single disordered state, yet low enough to allow biological switching on physiological timescales (milliseconds to hours). This requires a highly cooperative transition coordinate.

1.2.3 Continuous Free Energy Landscape Model

To formalize this mathematically for generative design, we model the free energy landscape $V(x)$ as a function of a continuous, low-dimensional collective variable $x$ (such as the difference in the fraction of native contacts $Q_{\mathcal{A}} - Q_{\mathcal{B}}$):

$$V(x) = E_0 \left( \left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right)^2 - 1 \right)^2 + \gamma \cdot \frac{x}{x_0}$$

where $E_0$ controls the intrinsic energy barrier between the two wells located near $-x_0$ (representing State $\mathcal{A}$) and $+x_0$ (representing State $\mathcal{B}$), and $\gamma$ is an asymmetry parameter representing the bias introduced by external chemical potentials, ligand binding, or environmental changes (such as pH or temperature):

$$\gamma \propto \Delta \mu_{\text{ligand}} = k_B T \ln \left(1 + \frac{[\text{Ligand}]}{K_d}\right)$$

1.3 Limitations of Traditional Static De Novo Design

Conventional computational protein design methodologies, exemplified by classical Rosetta design protocols, are fundamentally optimized for the "one sequence, one structure" paradigm. These limitations present severe bottlenecks when applied to metamorphic proteins.

Classical Design (Rosetta):
  Minimize E(S, Structure_A) ---> Highly stable Fold A, 
                                   but Fold B is ignored/highly unstable.

Metamorphic Design:
  Simultaneously satisfy:
    1. E(S, Structure_A) is low (stable)
    2. E(S, Structure_B) is low (stable)
    3. E(S, Transitions) does not lead to aggregation/misfolding.

1.3.1 The Negative Design Bottleneck

Traditional de novo design relies primarily on positive design: maximizing favorable interactions (hydrophobic packing, hydrogen bonding, salt bridges) for a single target backbone configuration. The energy score $E(\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{x})$ for sequence $\mathbf{s}$ on backbone conformation $\mathbf{x}$ is minimized:

$$\mathbf{s}^* = \arg\min_{\mathbf{s}} E(\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{x}_{\text{target}})$$

In metamorphic design, positive design must be applied simultaneously to two structurally incompatible backbones, $\mathbf{x}{\mathcal{A}}$ and $\mathbf{x}{\mathcal{B}}$. However, optimizing for $\mathbf{x}{\mathcal{A}}$ typically introduces severe steric clashes or exposed hydrophobic residues in the context of conformation $\mathbf{x}{\mathcal{B}}$, and vice versa.

Without explicit negative design (the destabilization of alternative, non-functional folds and aggregation-prone intermediate states), a dual-stabilized sequence will either:

  1. Collapse into a single, deeply trapped minimum (highly stable State $\mathcal{A}$, kinetic barrier to State $\mathcal{B}$ too high).
  2. Misfold into amorphous aggregates due to the high density of exposed hydrophobic patches required to satisfy both folds' core requirements.

1.3.2 Inductive Biases in Modern Machine Learning Models

State-of-the-art structural prediction networks (e.g., AlphaFold2, ESMFold) and generative backbone design models (e.g., RFdiffusion) are structurally biased toward static structures:

  • AlphaFold2's Recyclings and Evoformer: The network is trained to resolve structural ambiguity and output a single, high-confidence structure (minimizing the frame aligned error). The evolutionary signals in MSAs are collapsed to predict the most thermodynamically stable single conformer represented in the Protein Data Bank (PDB).
  • RFdiffusion: The denoising process operates on a single trajectory, diffusing random noise into a singular backbone coordinate system. It lacks the capacity to diffuse toward a dual-state density map or optimize for a bi-stable free energy landscape.

1.4 The Co-Evolutionary Landscape and Evolutionary Couplings (ECs)

When a protein must adapt to two distinct functional conformations, every residue position is subjected to a dual set of selective pressures. To maintain stability in State $\mathcal{A}$, residue $i$ must pack tightly against residue $j$. To maintain stability in State $\mathcal{B}$, residue $i$ must form a salt bridge with residue $k$.

This overlapping selective pressure leaves a distinct, mathematical imprint on the Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA) of the protein family. These patterns are extracted via Direct Coupling Analysis (DCA).

               Conformation A Contacts:       Conformation B Contacts:
                   (Residue i --- j)             (Residue i --- k)
                             \                         /
                              \                       /
                     Combined Co-evolutionary Signal in MSA:
                     - Co-varying positions: (i, j) AND (i, k)

1.4.1 Statistical Mechanics of MSAs: The Potts Model

To disentangle direct physical contacts from indirect correlations (e.g., if residue $A$ correlates with $B$, and $B$ with $C$, then $A$ correlates with $C$ without physical contact), we model the sequence distribution using a maximum entropy framework. Given an alignment, the probability of observing a specific sequence $\mathbf{s} = (s_1, s_2, \dots, s_L)$ of length $L$ is modeled by a pairwise Potts-like Hamiltonian:

$$P(\mathbf{s}) = \frac{1}{Z} \exp \left( -H(\mathbf{s}) \right) = \frac{1}{Z} \exp \left( \sum_{i=1}^L h_i(s_i) + \sum_{i < j}^{L} J_{ij}(s_i, s_j) \right)$$

where:

  • $s_i \in {1, \dots, 21}$ represents the amino acid (or gap) at position $i$.
  • $h_i(a)$ is a single-site field parameter bias favoring amino acid $a$ at position $i$ (reflecting conservation).
  • $J_{ij}(a, b)$ is the pairwise coupling parameter representing the direct co-evolutionary interaction between residue $i$ having amino acid $a$ and residue $j$ having amino acid $b$.
  • $Z$ is the partition function normalizing the probability distribution over all $21^L$ possible sequences.

1.4.2 Mathematical Optimization: Mean-Field and Pseudolikelihood Maximization

To determine the parameters $h_i$ and $J_{ij}$ from an empirical alignment, we must fit the model to match the first- and second-order marginal distributions of the MSA:

$$f_i(a) = \sum_{\mathbf{s}} P(\mathbf{s}) \delta(s_i, a)$$

$$f_{ij}(a, b) = \sum_{\mathbf{s}} P(\mathbf{s}) \delta(s_i, a) \delta(s_j, b)$$

Calculating $Z$ exactly is NP-hard. We therefore use two primary approximations:

1. Mean-Field DCA (mfDCA)

The mean-field approximation computes the coupling matrix through the inversion of the empirical correlation matrix $C$. Let:

$$C_{ij}(a, b) = f_{ij}(a, b) - f_i(a) f_j(b)$$

For a protein of length $L$, $C$ is a matrix of size $20L \times 20L$ (excluding the gap state to avoid over-parametrization). The coupling parameters are approximated directly as:

$$J_{ij}(a, b) = - (C^{-1})_{ij}(a, b)$$

2. Pseudolikelihood Maximization (PLM)

PLM is more computationally intensive but significantly more accurate. It approximates the joint likelihood of the sequences by the product of conditional probabilities of single sites given all other sites:

$$\mathcal{L}{\text{PLM}} = \prod{m=1}^{M} \prod_{i=1}^{L} P(s_{i,m} \mid \mathbf{s}_{-i,m})$$

$$P(s_i = a \mid \mathbf{s}{-i}) = \frac{\exp \left( h_i(a) + \sum{j \neq i} J_{ij}(a, s_j) \right)}{\sum_{c=1}^{21} \exp \left( h_i(c) + \sum_{j \neq i} J_{ij}(c, s_j) \right)}$$

The parameters $\mathbf{h}$ and $\mathbf{J}$ are estimated by minimizing the regularized negative log-pseudolikelihood:

$$\min_{\mathbf{h}, \mathbf{J}} \left( -\sum_{m=1}^M \sum_{i=1}^L \ln P(s_{i,m} \mid \mathbf{s}_{-i,m}) + \lambda_h |\mathbf{h}|_2^2 + \lambda_J |\mathbf{J}|_2^2 \right)$$

where $\lambda_h$ and $\lambda_j$ are L2 regularization parameters preventing overfitting on shallow alignments.

1.4.3 Deconvolution of Dual-State Evolutionary Matrices

In a static protein, the highest scoring evolutionary couplings $J_{ij}$ map directly to a single tertiary contact map. For metamorphic proteins, the coupling matrix $J_{ij}$ is a superposition of two structural states:

$$J_{ij}(a, b) \approx w_{\mathcal{A}} J_{ij}^{\mathcal{A}}(a, b) + w_{\mathcal{B}} J_{ij}^{\mathcal{B}}(a, b)$$

By analyzing the coupling matrix, we can identify mutually exclusive contact patterns:

  • State $\mathcal{A}$-specific contacts: Residue pairs $(i, j)$ that form contacts only in Fold $\mathcal{A}$.
  • State $\mathcal{B}$-specific contacts: Residue pairs $(i, k)$ that form contacts only in Fold $\mathcal{B}$.
  • Common contacts: Residue pairs $(y, z)$ that maintain their relative positioning in both folds (e.g., within rigid-body domain boundaries).
                      Evolutionary Coupling Matrix (J_ij)
                      
                         1              j              k             L
                      1  .              .              .             .
                         .   [State A]  .              .             .
                         .   (i-j pair) .              .             .
                      i  .------[*]-----.------[*]-----.             .
                         .              .    [State B] .             .
                         .              .    (i-k pair).             .
                      L  .              .              .             .

By decomposing the $J_{ij}$ matrix, a generative latent diffusion model can learn to alternate between these overlapping contact matrices, utilizing the co-evolutionary signals as prior constraints to guide the sampling of metamorphic sequences.

1.5 Python Architecture: Mean-Field DCA & Mutually Exclusive Contact Detection

The following self-contained, highly optimized Python program implements a Mean-Field Direct Coupling Analysis (mfDCA) pipeline. It generates a mock synthetic Multiple Sequence Alignment containing dual-state structural signals (State $\mathcal{A}$ and State $\mathcal{B}$) and subsequently extracts, deconvolves, and scores the co-evolutionary couplings to identify mutually exclusive contacts.

import numpy as np
import scipy.linalg as la

class MeanFieldDCA:
    """
    Implements Mean-Field Direct Coupling Analysis (mfDCA) to detect
    overlapping structural signatures from multi-state evolutionary alignments.
    """
    def __init__(self, msa_file: str = None, toy_data: bool = True):
        self.alphabet = "ACDEFGHIKLMNPQRSTVWY-"  # 20 amino acids + 1 gap
        self.q = len(self.alphabet)               # 21 states
        if toy_data:
            self.msa = self._generate_toy_msa(num_sequences=2000, sequence_length=30)
        else:
            self.msa = self._load_msa(msa_file)
        self.N, self.L = self.msa.shape
        print(f"Loaded MSA with {self.N} sequences and length {self.L}.")

    def _generate_toy_msa(self, num_sequences: int, sequence_length: int) -> np.ndarray:
        """
        Generates a synthetic MSA simulating a metamorphic protein.
        Introduces strong co-evolutionary couplings for two distinct states:
        State A: Contact between position 5 and 15
        State B: Contact between position 5 and 25
        """
        np.random.seed(42)
        # Initialize randomly from 21-state alphabet
        msa = np.random.randint(0, self.q, size=(num_sequences, sequence_length))
        
        # We will inject co-evolutionary signals
        # Position 5 (Valine [17] <-> Lysine [8]) co-varies with:
        # State A: Position 15 (Aspartate [3] <-> Glutamate [4])
        # State B: Position 25 (Arginine [14] <-> Threonine [15])
        
        for i in range(num_sequences):
            state_selector = np.random.rand()
            if state_selector < 0.5:
                # State A active co-evolution
                val_or_lys = np.random.choice([8, 17])
                msa[i, 5] = val_or_lys
                if val_or_lys == 8:  # Lys (+) -> Asp (-)
                    msa[i, 15] = 3
                else:                # Val (phobic) -> Glu (charged, neutral pairing)
                    msa[i, 15] = 4
            else:
                # State B active co-evolution
                val_or_lys = np.random.choice([8, 17])
                msa[i, 5] = val_or_lys
                if val_or_lys == 8:  # Lys (+) -> Arg (+) [Repulsion, engineered covariance]
                    msa[i, 25] = 14
                else:
                    msa[i, 25] = 15
        return msa

    def _load_msa(self, filename: str) -> np.ndarray:
        # Standard parser for FASTA alignments map to [0, 20] integers
        pass

    def compute_frequencies(self, pseudocount_weight: float = 0.5) -> tuple:
        """
        Calculates reweighted single-site and pairwise frequencies 
        with pseudocount corrections to prevent numerical singularities.
        """
        # 1. Simple weight calculation (identity threshold = 80%)
        # For simplicity in this script, we treat sequences with equal weights
        weights = np.ones(self.N)
        M_eff = np.sum(weights)
        
        # 2. Compute raw single-site marginals
        f_i = np.zeros((self.L, self.q))
        for i in range(self.L):
            for r in range(self.q):
                f_i[i, r] = np.sum(weights[self.msa[:, i] == r]) / M_eff

        # 3. Compute raw pairwise marginals
        f_ij = np.zeros((self.L, self.L, self.q, self.q))
        for i in range(self.L):
            for j in range(self.L):
                if i == j:
                    continue
                for r in range(self.q):
                    for s in range(self.q):
                        mask = (self.msa[:, i] == r) & (self.msa[:, j] == s)
                        f_ij[i, j, r, s] = np.sum(weights[mask]) / M_eff

        # 4. Apply Pseudocount regularization
        # f_pseudocount = (1 - lambda) * f + lambda / q
        lam = pseudocount_weight
        f_i_corr = (1 - lam) * f_i + lam / self.q
        
        f_ij_corr = np.zeros_like(f_ij)
        for i in range(self.L):
            for j in range(self.L):
                if i == j:
                    # Identity matrices on block diagonal
                    f_ij_corr[i, j] = (1 - lam) * f_ij[i, j] + lam / (self.q * self.q)
                    for r in range(self.q):
                        f_ij_corr[i, j, r, r] = (1 - lam) * f_i[i, r] + lam / self.q
                else:
                    f_ij_corr[i, j] = (1 - lam) * f_ij[i, j] + lam / (self.q * self.q)
                    
        return f_i_corr, f_ij_corr

    def compute_couplings(self) -> np.ndarray:
        """
        Solves for the inverse covariance matrix to isolate direct couplings J_ij.
        """
        f_i, f_ij = self.compute_frequencies()
        
        # Construct the covariance matrix C of size [L*(q-1) x L*(q-1)]
        # We drop the 21st state (gap) to remove gauge redundancy
        qm1 = self.q - 1
        C = np.zeros((self.L * qm1, self.L * qm1))
        
        for i in range(self.L):
            for j in range(self.L):
                for r in range(qm1):
                    for s in range(qm1):
                        idx_i = i * qm1 + r
                        idx_j = j * qm1 + s
                        C[idx_i, idx_j] = f_ij[i, j, r, s] - f_i[i, r] * f_j[j, s]
                        
        # Invert the covariance matrix using LU decomposition / inversion
        print("Inverting Covariance Matrix to solve Potts model...")
        invC = la.inv(C)
        
        # Extract couplings J_ij
        J = np.zeros((self.L, self.L, self.q, self.q))
        for i in range(self.L):
            for j in range(self.L):
                if i == j:
                    continue
                for r in range(qm1):
                    for s in range(qm1):
                        idx_i = i * qm1 + r
                        idx_j = j * qm1 + s
                        # The coupling is the negative inverse covariance element
                        J[i, j, r, s] = -invC[idx_i, idx_j]
        return J

    def compute_frobenius_scores(self, J: np.ndarray) -> np.ndarray:
        """
        Computes the Frobenius norm for each pair (i, j) with gauge zero-sum correction,
        then applies Average Product Correction (APC) to eliminate phylogenetic bias.
        """
        F = np.zeros((self.L, self.L))
        qm1 = self.q - 1
        
        # 1. Compute raw Frobenius norm of coupling matrices J_ij (excluding gaps)
        for i in range(self.L):
            for j in range(self.L):
                if i >= j:
                    continue
                # Shift to Zero-Sum Gauge (Ising gauge approximation)
                sub_matrix = J[i, j, :qm1, :qm1]
                row_means = np.mean(sub_matrix, axis=1, keepdims=True)
                col_means = np.mean(sub_matrix, axis=0, keepdims=True)
                grand_mean = np.mean(sub_matrix)
                gauge_corrected = sub_matrix - row_means - col_means + grand_mean
                
                f_norm = np.linalg.norm(gauge_corrected, 'fro')
                F[i, j] = f_norm
                F[j, i] = f_norm

        # 2. Apply Average Product Correction (APC)
        F_apc = np.zeros((self.L, self.L))
        mean_row = np.sum(F, axis=1) / (self.L - 1)
        mean_all = np.sum(F) / (self.L * (self.L - 1))
        
        for i in range(self.L):
            for j in range(self.L):
                if i == j:
                    continue
                F_apc[i, j] = F[i, j] - (mean_row[i] * mean_row[j]) / mean_all
                if F_apc[i, j] < 0:
                    F_apc[i, j] = 0.0
                    
        return F_apc

    def deconvolve_multistate_contacts(self, F_apc: np.ndarray, threshold: float = 0.08) -> dict:
        """
        Delineates active co-evolutionary nodes representing distinct states.
        """
        detected_contacts = []
        for i in range(self.L):
            for j in range(i + 1, self.L):
                if F_apc[i, j] > threshold:
                    detected_contacts.append((i, j, F_apc[i, j]))
        
        # Sort by co-evolutionary strength
        detected_contacts.sort(key=lambda x: x[2], reverse=True)
        
        # Identify branching evolutionary hubs (indicative of metamorphic residues)
        hubs = {}
        for i, j, score in detected_contacts:
            hubs.setdefault(i, []).append((j, score))
            hubs.setdefault(j, []).append((i, score))
            
        return {
            "top_contacts": detected_contacts[:10],
            "hubs": {k: v for k, v in hubs.items() if len(v) >= 2}
        }

if __name__ == "__main__":
    dca = MeanFieldDCA(toy_data=True)
    J_matrices = dca.compute_couplings()
    contact_scores = dca.compute_frobenius_scores(J_matrices)
    results = dca.deconvolve_multistate_contacts(contact_scores)
    
    print("\n=== TOP CO-EVOLUTIONARY CONTACTS IDENTIFIED ===")
    for rank, (i, j, score) in enumerate(results["top_contacts"], 1):
        print(f"Rank {rank}: Position {i:02d} <---> Position {j:02d} [APC Score: {score:.4f}]")
        
    print("\n=== DETECTED METAMORPHIC EVOLUTIONARY HUBS ===")
    for hub, targets in results["hubs"].items():
        targets_str = ", ".join([f"Pos {t} (Score: {sc:.2f})" for t, sc in targets])
        print(f"Hub Residue [Pos {hub:02d}] is coupled to multiple positions: {targets_str}")

1.6.1 Mathematical Formulation of Guided Latent Diffusion

The generation process utilizes conditional denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPM). Let $\mathbf{z}_t$ represent the latent variables of the protein structure at denoising step $t \in [0, T]$. The conditional reverse diffusion step is modeled as:

$$p_\theta(\mathbf{z}{t-1} \mid \mathbf{z}t, \mathbf{C}^{\mathcal{A}}, \mathbf{C}^{\mathcal{B}}) = \mathcal{N}\left(\mathbf{z}{t-1}; \mathbf{\mu}\theta(\mathbf{z}t, \mathbf{C}^{\mathcal{A}}, \mathbf{C}^{\mathcal{B}}), \mathbf{\Sigma}\theta(\mathbf{z}_t)\right)$$

The score function (or mean $\mathbf{\mu}_\theta$) is adjusted by the gradient of a structural classifier or guide that enforces compatibility with both contact matrices $\mathbf{C}^{\mathcal{A}}$ and $\mathbf{C}^{\mathcal{B}}$:

$$\mathbf{\mu}_\theta(\mathbf{z}t, \mathbf{C}^{\mathcal{A}}, \mathbf{C}^{\mathcal{B}}) = \mathbf{\mu}\theta(\mathbf{z}t) + s \cdot \nabla{\mathbf{z}t} \left( \mathcal{L}{\text{contact}}(\mathbf{z}t, \mathbf{C}^{\mathcal{A}}) + \mathcal{L}{\text{contact}}(\mathbf{z}_t, \mathbf{C}^{\mathcal{B}}) \right)$$

where $s$ is a scaling factor balancing standard structural feasibility against the dual-state topological constraints derived from our evolutionary analysis.

1. Mathematical Foundations of Co-Evolutionary Latent Spaces

1.1 Direct Coupling Analysis (DCA) and the Potts Model

The co-evolutionary signals within a Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA) of homologous proteins are captured by modeling the joint probability distribution of sequence variations. Let a protein sequence of length $L$ be represented as $\boldsymbol{\sigma} = (\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \dots, \sigma_L)$, where each residue $\sigma_i$ belongs to an alphabet $\mathcal{A}$ of $21$ symbols (20 amino acids plus 1 gap).

The global probability distribution $P(\boldsymbol{\sigma})$ that maximizes entropy while matching observed single-site and pairwise amino acid frequencies is given by the Potts model:

$$P(\boldsymbol{\sigma}) = \frac{1}{Z} \exp \left( \sum_{i=1}^{L} h_i(\sigma_i) + \sum_{1 \le i < j \le L} J_{ij}(\sigma_i, \sigma_j) \right)$$

where:

  • $Z$ is the partition function: $$Z = \sum_{\boldsymbol{\sigma} \in \mathcal{A}^L} \exp \left( \sum_{i=1}^{L} h_i(\sigma_i) + \sum_{1 \le i < j \le L} J_{ij}(\sigma_i, \sigma_j) \right)$$
  • $h_i(\sigma_i) \in \mathbb{R}^{21}$ represents the local single-site fields, capturing site-specific conservation.
  • $J_{ij}(\sigma_i, \sigma_j) \in \mathbb{R}^{21 \times 21}$ represents the direct coupling parameters between residues at position $i$ and $j$, isolating direct epistatic co-evolutionary constraints from indirect correlations.

To obtain a scalar representation of the evolutionary coupling strength between sites $i$ and $j$, we compute the Frobenius norm of the coupling matrix $J_{ij}$ for all amino acid pairs, excluding gaps:

$$S_{ij} = | J_{ij} |{F} = \sqrt{\sum{a, b \in \mathcal{A} \setminus {-}} J_{ij}(a, b)^2}$$

To correct for phylogenetic bias and high entropy sites, we apply the Average Product Correction (APC):

$$F_{ij} = S_{ij} - \frac{S_{i\cdot} S_{\cdot j}}{S_{\cdot\cdot}}$$

where $S_{i\cdot} = \frac{1}{L}\sum_{k=1}^L S_{ik}$, $S_{\cdot j} = \frac{1}{L}\sum_{k=1}^L S_{kj}$, and $S_{\cdot\cdot} = \frac{1}{L^2}\sum_{g=1}^L\sum_{h=1}^L S_{gh}$. The resulting matrix $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times L}$ is the Evolutionary Coupling (EC) matrix, representing spatial proximity constraints across the evolutionary history of the protein family.

1.2 Variational Autoencoding of Coupling Manifolds

Direct generation of EC matrices using standard generative models is computationally challenging due to structural constraints (e.g., symmetry, positive semi-definiteness properties of distance matrices, and sparsity). We resolve this by mapping $\mathbf{X}$ to a lower-dimensional, continuous latent space $\mathcal{Z} = \mathbb{R}^{d}$ ($d \ll L \times L$) using a Variational Autoencoder (VAE).

Let $E_\phi(\mathbf{X})$ denote the probabilistic encoder parameterized by weights $\phi$, and $D_\psi(\mathbf{z})$ denote the decoder parameterized by weights $\psi$. The encoder maps the input matrix $\mathbf{X}$ to a latent distribution $q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{X}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{z}; \boldsymbol{\mu}\phi(\mathbf{X}), \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\phi(\mathbf{X}))$, where $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\phi(\mathbf{X}) = \operatorname{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}\phi^2(\mathbf{X}))$.

The decoder maps a latent vector $\mathbf{z} \in \mathcal{Z}$ back to the reconstructed coupling matrix $\hat{\mathbf{X}} = D_\psi(\mathbf{z})$. The network parameters ${\phi, \psi}$ are optimized by minimizing the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO):

$$\mathcal{L}{\text{VAE}}(\phi, \psi; \mathbf{X}) = \mathbb{E}{q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{X})} \left[ -\log p_\psi(\mathbf{X}|\mathbf{z}) \right] + D_{\text{KL}}\left(q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{X}) \parallel p(\mathbf{z})\right)$$

The reconstruction loss $-\log p_\psi(\mathbf{X}|\mathbf{z})$ is formulated as a weighted mean squared error to prioritize short-to-medium range contacts:

$$\mathcal{L}{\text{recon}} = \sum{i < j} w_{ij} \left( F_{ij} - [D_\psi(\mathbf{z})]_{ij} \right)^2$$

where $w_{ij} = |i - j|^{-\gamma}$ is a distance-dependent weight parameter ($\gamma \in [0.5, 1.0]$) that balances the focus between local secondary structures and long-range tertiary interactions. The prior distribution $p(\mathbf{z})$ is defined as a standard multivariate Gaussian, $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$.

2. Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) on Evolutionary Manifolds

Once the continuous, low-dimensional latent space $\mathcal{Z}$ is established, we construct a diffusion process over this manifold. Navigating the latent space instead of the high-dimensional coupling space reduces computational complexity and prevents the generation of structurally unfeasible artifacts.

z_0 <─────────────────────────────────────────── z_t <─────────────────────────────────────────── z_T

2.1 The Forward Diffusion Process

The forward diffusion process progressively corrupts the latent representation $\mathbf{z}0 = E\phi(\mathbf{X})$ by adding Gaussian noise over $T$ discrete time steps according to a predefined variance schedule $\beta_1, \beta_2, \dots, \beta_T$:

$$q(\mathbf{z}_{1:T} | \mathbf{z}0) = \prod{t=1}^{T} q(\mathbf{z}t | \mathbf{z}{t-1})$$

$$q(\mathbf{z}t | \mathbf{z}{t-1}) = \mathcal{N}\left(\mathbf{z}t; \sqrt{1 - \beta_t}\mathbf{z}{t-1}, \beta_t \mathbf{I}\right)$$

Using the notation $\alpha_t = 1 - \beta_t$ and $\bar{\alpha}t = \prod{s=1}^t \alpha_s$, we can sample the latent vector at any arbitrary time step $t$ directly from $\mathbf{z}_0$:

$$q(\mathbf{z}_t | \mathbf{z}_0) = \mathcal{N}\left(\mathbf{z}_t; \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\mathbf{z}_0, (1 - \bar{\alpha}_t)\mathbf{I}\right)$$

This allows us to write the explicit parameterization:

$$\mathbf{z}_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\mathbf{z}_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t}\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$$

where $\boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$.

2.2 The Reverse Process and Denoising Score Matching

The generative engine learns to invert the forward corruption process. The reverse transition probability distribution $p_\theta(\mathbf{z}_{t-1} | \mathbf{z}_t)$ is parameterized by a neural network:

$$p_\theta(\mathbf{z}{0:T}) = p(\mathbf{z}T) \prod{t=1}^{T} p\theta(\mathbf{z}_{t-1} | \mathbf{z}_t)$$

$$p_\theta(\mathbf{z}{t-1} | \mathbf{z}t) = \mathcal{N}\left(\mathbf{z}{t-1}; \boldsymbol{\mu}\theta(\mathbf{z}t, t), \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\theta(\mathbf{z}_t, t)\right)$$

The mean parameterization is defined as:

$$\boldsymbol{\mu}_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t, t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}} \left( \mathbf{z}_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}t}} \boldsymbol{\epsilon}\theta(\mathbf{z}_t, t, \mathbf{c}) \right)$$

where $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t, t, \mathbf{c})$ is a denoising autoencoder parameterized by $\theta$. It is trained to predict the added noise vector $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ given the noisy latent vector $\mathbf{z}_t$, time step $t$, and a conditioning vector $\mathbf{c}$ encoding the target biophysical properties:

$$\mathcal{L}{\text{LDM}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}{\mathbf{z}0, \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}), t} \left[ \left| \boldsymbol{\epsilon} - \boldsymbol{\epsilon}\theta\left(\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\mathbf{z}_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t}\boldsymbol{\epsilon}, t, \mathbf{c}\right) \right|^2_2 \right]$$

2.3 Conditional Classifier-Free Guidance for Dual-State Topologies

For metamorphic proteins, the conditioning vector $\mathbf{c}$ must specify two target topologies, $\tau_A$ and $\tau_B$. To control the generated outputs, we use Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG). During training, the conditioning vector $\mathbf{c} = (\tau_A, \tau_B)$ is randomly replaced with a null token $\emptyset$ with probability $p_{\text{uncond}} \in [0.1, 0.2]$.

During generation, the noise prediction is interpolated to control the influence of the target topologies:

$$\tilde{\boldsymbol{\epsilon}}_\theta(\mathbf{z}t, t, \mathbf{c}) = (1 + w)\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\theta(\mathbf{z}t, t, \mathbf{c}) - w\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\theta(\mathbf{z}_t, t, \emptyset)$$

where $w \ge 0$ is the guidance scale parameter.

  • High values of $w$ ($w > 4$) force the generated evolutionary coupling patterns to strictly align with both input target structures, potentially reducing sequence diversity.
  • Intermediate values ($1 < w \le 3$) allow the model to explore new co-evolutionary patterns that bridge the target structures.

3.1 Multi-Scale Spatial VAE

The input EC matrix $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times L \times 1}$ is processed using hierarchical 2D convolutional layers. The encoder downsamples the matrix spatially by a factor of 8 while increasing the channel dimension:

$$\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times L \times 1} \xrightarrow{\text{Encoder}} \mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{\frac{L}{8} \times \frac{L}{8} \times d_z}$$

The spatial grid structure preserves the 2D layout of residues in physical space. The encoder $E_\phi$ consists of four downsampling blocks, each utilizing ResNet blocks with Group Normalization and Swish activations:

$$\operatorname{ResBlock}(\mathbf{h}) = \mathbf{h} + \operatorname{Conv2D}\left(\operatorname{Swish}\left(\operatorname{GroupNorm}\left(\operatorname{Conv2D}\left(\operatorname{Swish}\left(\operatorname{GroupNorm}(\mathbf{h})\right)\right)\right)\right)\right)$$

The spatial decoder $D_\psi$ mirrors this structure, using bilinear upsampling layers to reconstruct the original matrix dimension:

$$\hat{\mathbf{X}} = D_\psi(\mathbf{z}) \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times L \times 1}$$

3.2 Latent Diffusion Transformer (DiT) Block

Instead of using a standard 2D U-Net, we process the latent space representations using a Diffusion Transformer (DiT) to better capture long-range dependencies in the co-evolutionary matrix.

  1. Patchification: The latent variable $\mathbf{z}t \in \mathbb{R}^{\frac{L}{8} \times \frac{L}{8} \times d_z}$ is divided into $P \times P$ spatial patches. These patches are flattened and linearly projected into a sequence of tokens $\mathbf{T} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d{\text{model}}}$, where $N = \left(\frac{L}{8P}\right)^2$.
  2. Position Embedding: Standard learnable 2D sinusoidal position embeddings $\mathbf{E}{\text{pos}} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d{\text{model}}}$ are added to the token sequence: $$\mathbf{T}0 = \mathbf{T} + \mathbf{E}{\text{pos}}$$
  3. Conditioning Injection: The conditioning vector $\mathbf{c}$ and the diffusion time step $t$ are projected into conditioning tokens $\mathbf{C}k \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times d{\text{model}}}$.
  4. Multi-Head Cross-Attention (MHCA): In each transformer block, sequence tokens query the conditioning tokens: $$\operatorname{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \operatorname{softmax}\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^T}{\sqrt{d_{\text{head}}}}\right)\mathbf{V}$$ where $\mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{T}_l \mathbf{W}_Q$, $\mathbf{K} = \mathbf{C}_k \mathbf{W}_K$, and $\mathbf{V} = \mathbf{C}_k \mathbf{W}_V$.

4. Encoding Dual-State Fold Topologies (Metamorphism)

4.1 Biophysical Target and Loss Formulations

For a metamorphic sequence to fold into two distinct conformations, state A and state B, its evolutionary coupling matrix must contain the contact patterns of both folds. This joint pattern is not a simple average, which could lead to physical conflicts, but an optimized overlapping set of contact constraints.

Let $\mathbf{C}^A \in {0, 1}^{L \times L}$ and $\mathbf{C}^B \in {0, 1}^{L \times L}$ represent the binary contact maps of state A and state B respectively, derived from their PDB coordinates using an $8,\text{Å}$ $\text{C}\beta-\text{C}\beta$ distance threshold.

       State A Contact Map                       State B Contact Map
      +---------------------+                   +---------------------+
      |  *                  |                   |        *            |
      |    *                |                   |          *          |
      |      *   (Domain 1) |                   |            * (Domain |
      |        *            |                   |              *   2) |
      +---------------------+                   +---------------------+
                 \                                         /
                  \                                       /
                   ▼                                     ▼
                  +---------------------------------------+
                  |  *     *   (Dual-State Overlap)       |
                  |    *     *                            |
                  |      *     *                          |
                  |        *     *                        |
                  +---------------------------------------+
                     Target Multi-State Structural Loss (L_str)

To guide the generative model, we define a multi-state structural conditioning loss $\mathcal{L}{\text{str}}$ over the decoded coupling matrix $\hat{\mathbf{X}} = D\psi(\mathbf{z}_0)$:

$$\mathcal{L}{\text{str}}(\hat{\mathbf{X}}, \mathbf{C}^A, \mathbf{C}^B) = \lambda_A \mathcal{D}{\text{BCE}}(\hat{\mathbf{X}} \odot \mathbf{M}^A, \mathbf{C}^A) + \lambda_B \mathcal{D}{\text{BCE}}(\hat{\mathbf{X}} \odot \mathbf{M}^B, \mathbf{C}^B) + \gamma \mathcal{L}{\text{exclusion}}(\hat{\mathbf{X}})$$

where:

  • $\mathcal{D}_{\text{BCE}}$ is the Binary Cross-Entropy loss computed over the predicted contact probabilities.
  • $\mathbf{M}^A$ and $\mathbf{M}^B$ are masks highlighting regions of structural divergence between the two topologies.
  • $\lambda_A, \lambda_B$ are dynamic weight factors balancing the thermodynamic stability of both states.
  • $\mathcal{L}{\text{exclusion}}(\hat{\mathbf{X}})$ is an exclusion penalty preventing steric clashes. It penalizes co-evolutionary signals that require the same residue to project into conflicting structural positions simultaneously without a corresponding state transition: $$\mathcal{L}{\text{exclusion}}(\hat{\mathbf{X}}) = \sum_{i} \operatorname{ReLU}\left( \sum_{j} \hat{X}{ij} \cdot \mathbb{I}\left( d{ij}^A < 5.0 \land d_{ij}^B > 15.0 \right) - \theta_{\text{max}} \right)$$

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F
import math
from typing import Tuple, Dict, Optional

class ResNetBlock2D(nn.Module):
    """
    Standard ResNet Block used in Spatial Encoder/Decoder for processing
    Evolutionary Coupling matrices.
    """
    def __init__(self, channels: int):
        super().__init__()
        self.conv1 = nn.Conv2d(channels, channels, kernel_size=3, padding=1)
        self.gn1 = nn.GroupNorm(num_groups=4, num_channels=channels)
        self.conv2 = nn.Conv2d(channels, channels, kernel_size=3, padding=1)
        self.gn2 = nn.GroupNorm(num_groups=4, num_channels=channels)

    def forward(self, x: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        residual = x
        x = self.gn1(x)
        x = F.silu(x)
        x = self.conv1(x)
        x = self.gn2(x)
        x = F.silu(x)
        x = self.conv2(x)
        return x + residual

class SpatialVAE(nn.Module):
    """
    Spatial VAE mapping L x L evolutionary coupling matrices to
    (L/8) x (L/8) x d_z continuous latent variables.
    """
    def __init__(self, in_channels: int = 1, latent_channels: int = 8, base_channels: int = 32):
        super().__init__()
        self.latent_channels = latent_channels
        
        # Encoder: Downsamples input by 8
        self.enc_init = nn.Conv2d(in_channels, base_channels, kernel_size=3, padding=1)
        self.down1 = nn.Conv2d(base_channels, base_channels * 2, kernel_size=4, stride=2, padding=1) # L/2
        self.down2 = nn.Conv2d(base_channels * 2, base_channels * 4, kernel_size=4, stride=2, padding=1) # L/4
        self.down3 = nn.Conv2d(base_channels * 4, base_channels * 8, kernel_size=4, stride=2, padding=1) # L/8
        
        self.enc_res = ResNetBlock2D(base_channels * 8)
        self.to_moments = nn.Conv2d(base_channels * 8, latent_channels * 2, kernel_size=1)
        
        # Decoder: Upsamples latent by 8
        self.from_latent = nn.Conv2d(latent_channels, base_channels * 8, kernel_size=1)
        self.dec_res = ResNetBlock2D(base_channels * 8)
        
        self.up1 = nn.ConvTranspose2d(base_channels * 8, base_channels * 4, kernel_size=4, stride=2, padding=1)
        self.up2 = nn.ConvTranspose2d(base_channels * 4, base_channels * 2, kernel_size=4, stride=2, padding=1)
        self.up3 = nn.ConvTranspose2d(base_channels * 2, base_channels, kernel_size=4, stride=2, padding=1)
        self.dec_out = nn.Conv2d(base_channels, in_channels, kernel_size=3, padding=1)

    def encode(self, x: torch.Tensor) -> Tuple[torch.Tensor, torch.Tensor]:
        h = F.silu(self.enc_init(x))
        h = F.silu(self.down1(h))
        h = F.silu(self.down2(h))
        h = F.silu(self.down3(h))
        h = self.enc_res(h)
        moments = self.to_moments(h)
        mean, logvar = torch.chunk(moments, 2, dim=1)
        logvar = torch.clamp(logvar, -30.0, 20.0)
        return mean, logvar

    def reparameterize(self, mean: torch.Tensor, logvar: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        std = torch.exp(0.5 * logvar)
        eps = torch.randn_like(std)
        return mean + eps * std

    def decode(self, z: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        h = self.from_latent(z)
        h = self.dec_res(h)
        h = F.silu(self.up1(h))
        h = F.silu(self.up2(h))
        h = F.silu(self.up3(h))
        return torch.sigmoid(self.dec_out(h))

    def forward(self, x: torch.Tensor) -> Tuple[torch.Tensor, torch.Tensor, torch.Tensor]:
        mean, logvar = self.encode(x)
        z = self.reparameterize(mean, logvar)
        x_recon = self.decode(z)
        return x_recon, mean, logvar

class SinusoidalPositionEmbedding(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, dim: int):
        super().__init__()
        self.dim = dim

    def forward(self, timesteps: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        device = timesteps.device
        half_dim = self.dim // 2
        embeddings = math.log(10000) / (half_dim - 1)
        embeddings = torch.exp(torch.arange(half_dim, device=device) * -embeddings)
        embeddings = timesteps[:, None] * embeddings[None, :]
        embeddings = torch.cat((embeddings.sin(), embeddings.cos()), dim=-1)
        return embeddings

class DiTBlock(nn.Module):
    """
    Diffusion Transformer Block supporting dual cross-attention to inject
    structural targets.
    """
    def __init__(self, d_model: int, n_heads: int):
        super().__init__()
        self.n_heads = n_heads
        self.d_model = d_model
        
        # Self-attention over latent patches
        self.attn_norm = nn.LayerNorm(d_model)
        self.self_attn = nn.MultiheadAttention(d_model, n_heads, batch_first=True)
        
        # Cross-attention over structural conditions
        self.cross_norm = nn.LayerNorm(d_model)
        self.cross_attn = nn.MultiheadAttention(d_model, n_heads, batch_first=True)
        
        # Feedforward network
        self.ffn_norm = nn.LayerNorm(d_model)
        self.ffn = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(d_model, d_model * 4),
            nn.SiLU(),
            nn.Linear(d_model * 4, d_model)
        )

    def forward(self, x: torch.Tensor, cond: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        # Self-attention step
        norm_x = self.attn_norm(x)
        attn_out, _ = self.self_attn(norm_x, norm_x, norm_x)
        x = x + attn_out
        
        # Cross-attention step (queries = latent tokens, keys/values = conditioning tokens)
        norm_x = self.cross_norm(x)
        cross_out, _ = self.cross_attn(norm_x, cond, cond)
        x = x + cross_out
        
        # FFN step
        x = x + self.ffn(self.ffn_norm(x))
        return x

class LatentDiffusionTransformer(nn.Module):
    """
    Latent Diffusion Transformer (DiT) predicting noise in the spatial
    latent representation of the VAE under dual structural conditioning.
    """
    def __init__(self, latent_channels: int = 8, d_model: int = 128, n_heads: int = 8, depth: int = 6):
        super().__init__()
        self.d_model = d_model
        self.latent_channels = latent_channels
        
        # Patch projection (Processes 2x2 patches in the latent grid)
        self.patch_size = 2
        self.patch_proj = nn.Linear(latent_channels * (self.patch_size ** 2), d_model)
        
        # Positional and time embeddings
        self.time_embed = nn.Sequential(
            SinusoidalPositionEmbedding(d_model),
            nn.Linear(d_model, d_model),
            nn.SiLU()
        )
        
        # Projection of structural conditioning matrices
        self.cond_proj = nn.Linear(128, d_model) # Assumes 1D summary sequence feature vector of length 128
        
        # Main Transformer blocks
        self.blocks = nn.ModuleList([
            DiTBlock(d_model, n_heads) for _ in range(depth)
        ])
        
        self.final_norm = nn.LayerNorm(d_model)
        self.final_proj = nn.Linear(d_model, latent_channels * (self.patch_size ** 2))

    def _patchify(self, x: torch.Tensor) -> Tuple[torch.Tensor, int, int]:
        B, C, H, W = x.shape
        p = self.patch_size
        assert H % p == 0 and W % p == 0, f"Latent dimensions ({H}x{W}) must be divisible by patch size {p}"
        
        # Reshape to (B, C, H_patches, p, W_patches, p)
        h_p, w_p = H // p, W // p
        x = x.view(B, C, h_p, p, w_p, p)
        # Transpose and permute to (B, H_patches * W_patches, p * p * C)
        x = x.permute(0, 2, 4, 3, 5, 1).contiguous()
        x = x.view(B, h_p * w_p, p * p * C)
        return x, h_p, w_p

    def _unpatchify(self, x: torch.Tensor, h_p: int, w_p: int) -> torch.Tensor:
        B, N, patch_dim = x.shape
        p = self.patch_size
        C = self.latent_channels
        
        # Reverse projection sequence to (B, H_patches, W_patches, p, p, C)
        x = x.view(B, h_p, w_p, p, p, C)
        x = x.permute(0, 5, 1, 3, 2, 4).contiguous()
        x = x.view(B, C, h_p * p, w_p * p)
        return x

    def forward(self, z_t: torch.Tensor, t: torch.Tensor, c_A: torch.Tensor, c_B: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        # Patchify input
        patches, h_p, w_p = self._patchify(z_t)
        tokens = self.patch_proj(patches) # (B, N, d_model)
        
        # Prepare embeddings
        t_emb = self.time_embed(t).unsqueeze(1) # (B, 1, d_model)
        c_A_emb = self.cond_proj(c_A).unsqueeze(1) # (B, 1, d_model)
        c_B_emb = self.cond_proj(c_B).unsqueeze(1) # (B, 1, d_model)
        
        # Combine conditioning tokens
        cond = torch.cat([t_emb, c_A_emb, c_B_emb], dim=1) # (B, 3, d_model)
        
        # Apply Transformer blocks
        for block in self.blocks:
            tokens = block(tokens, cond)
            
        tokens = self.final_norm(tokens)
        out_patches = self.final_proj(tokens)
        
        # Project tokens back to the latent grid shape
        return self._unpatchify(out_patches, h_p, w_p)

class LatentDiffusionEngine(nn.Module):
    """
    Main manager for the training, sampling, and reverse execution
    of latent diffusion on co-evolutionary manifolds.
    """
    def __init__(self, vae: SpatialVAE, dit: LatentDiffusionTransformer, n_steps: int = 1000):
        super().__init__()
        self.vae = vae
        self.dit = dit
        self.n_steps = n_steps
        
        # Setup linear noise schedule
        self.beta = torch.linspace(1e-4, 0.02, n_steps)
        self.alpha = 1.0 - self.beta
        self.alpha_hat = torch.cumprod(self.alpha, dim=0)

    def get_alpha_hat(self, t: torch.Tensor, shape: torch.Size) -> torch.Tensor:
        # Move noise schedule tensors to the target device
        self.alpha_hat = self.alpha_hat.to(t.device)
        out = self.alpha_hat.gather(-1, t)
        return out.reshape(t.shape[0], *((1,) * (len(shape) - 1)))

    def q_sample(self, z_0: torch.Tensor, t: torch.Tensor, noise: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        """Forward diffusion step: corrupt the latent vector with noise."""
        alpha_hat_t = self.get_alpha_hat(t, z_0.shape)
        return torch.sqrt(alpha_hat_t) * z_0 + torch.sqrt(1.0 - alpha_hat_t) * noise

    def compute_loss(self, x: torch.Tensor, c_A: torch.Tensor, c_B: torch.Tensor) -> Dict[str, torch.Tensor]:
        """Runs VAE encoding, forward diffusion, and DiT prediction to calculate loss."""
        B, C, H, W = x.shape
        device = x.device
        
        # 1. Encode into continuous latent space
        with torch.no_grad():
            mean, logvar = self.vae.encode(x)
            z_0 = self.vae.reparameterize(mean, logvar)
            
        # 2. Sample noise and timesteps
        noise = torch.randn_like(z_0)
        t = torch.randint(0, self.n_steps, (B,), device=device).long()
        
        # 3. Apply noise to the latent representation
        z_t = self.q_sample(z_0, t, noise)
        
        # 4. Predict the noise using DiT
        predicted_noise = self.dit(z_t, t, c_A, c_B)
        
        # 5. Compute loss
        loss_diffusion = F.mse_loss(predicted_noise, noise)
        
        return {"loss": loss_diffusion}

    @torch.no_grad()
    def sample(self, c_A: torch.Tensor, c_B: torch.Tensor, spatial_dim: int = 64, cfg_scale: float = 2.0) -> torch.Tensor:
        """
        Samples a new evolutionary coupling matrix from the model
        using Classifier-Free Guidance.
        """
        device = c_A.device
        B = c_A.shape[0]
        latent_dim = spatial_dim // 8 # Decimated by spatial autoencoder
        
        # 1. Initialize latent representation with pure Gaussian noise
        z_t = torch.randn(B, self.vae.latent_channels, latent_dim, latent_dim, device=device)
        
        # Move noise schedule tensors to the target device
        self.alpha = self.alpha.to(device)
        self.beta = self.beta.to(device)
        self.alpha_hat = self.alpha_hat.to(device)

        # 2. Iterate backwards through the noise schedule
        for i in reversed(range(self.n_steps)):
            t = torch.full((B,), i, device=device, dtype=torch.long)
            
            # Predict noise under conditional guidance
            predicted_noise_cond = self.dit(z_t, t, c_A, c_B)
            
            if cfg_scale > 0:
                # To perform classifier-free guidance, run the model with unconditioned states (zeros)
                c_null = torch.zeros_like(c_A)
                predicted_noise_uncond = self.dit(z_t, t, c_null, c_null)
                # Interpolate between conditioned and unconditioned noise predictions
                predicted_noise = (1.0 + cfg_scale) * predicted_noise_cond - cfg_scale * predicted_noise_uncond
            else:
                predicted_noise = predicted_noise_cond
                
            alpha_t = self.alpha[i]
            alpha_hat_t = self.alpha_hat[i]
            beta_t = self.beta[i]
            
            if i > 0:
                noise = torch.randn_like(z_t)
            else:
                noise = torch.zeros_like(z_t)
                
            # Compute denoised latent representation z_{t-1}
            z_t = (1.0 / torch.sqrt(alpha_t)) * (z_t - ((beta_t / torch.sqrt(1.0 - alpha_hat_t)) * predicted_noise)) + torch.sqrt(beta_t) * noise
            
        # 3. Decode denoised latent back to full Evolutionary Coupling Matrix space
        generated_ec = self.vae.decode(z_t)
        return generated_ec

6. Verification and Structural Evaluation Metrics

To verify that the generated evolutionary coupling matrices accurately capture the target dual-state topologies, we evaluate them using three structural metrics.

       Generated EC Matrix                    Physical Topologies
     +---------------------+                 +---------------------+
     |                     |                 |      State A        |
     |   *   *             |                 |   (Active Folds)    |
     |     *     *         | ──────────────> +---------------------+
     |       *     *       | \ 
     +---------------------+  \              +---------------------+

                                             |  (Inactive Folds)   |

6.1 Multi-State Contact Precision

We compute the precision of the top $L/5$ predicted long-range contacts ($|i - j| \ge 12$) against both structural states. Let $\mathbf{P}_K$ be the set of the $K$ highest-valued elements in the generated coupling matrix $\hat{\mathbf{X}}$. The contact precision for state $S \in {A, B}$ is defined as:

$$\text{Precision}K^S = \frac{1}{K} \sum{(i,j) \in \mathbf{P}K} \mathbb{I}\left( d{ij}^S \le 8.0,\text{Å} \right)$$

where $d_{ij}^S$ is the true physical distance between residues $i$ and $j$ in conformation $S$. A valid metamorphic sequence must show high contact precision for both target states.

6.2 Topological Compatibility Score

The compatibility of the generated coupling matrix $\hat{\mathbf{X}}$ with both physical folds is evaluated using a normalized alignment score:

$$\Gamma(\hat{\mathbf{X}}; \mathbf{C}^A, \mathbf{C}^B) = \frac{\sum_{i < j} \hat{X}{ij} \left( C{ij}^A + C_{ij}^B \right)}{\sqrt{\sum_{i < j} \left(C_{ij}^A\right)^2 + \sum_{i < j} \left(C_{ij}^B\right)^2}}$$

This metric measures how well the generated evolutionary couplings overlap with the structural contact maps of both target conformations. High values indicate a sequence pattern optimized for both folds.

6.3 Fréchet Couplings Distance (FCD)

To evaluate the overall quality and distribution of the generated co-evolutionary patterns compared to natural MSAs, we define the Fréchet Couplings Distance (FCD). This metric is modeled after the Fréchet Inception Distance used in image generation:

$$\text{FCD} = |\boldsymbol{\mu}_r - \boldsymbol{\mu}_g|_2^2 + \operatorname{Tr}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_r + \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_g - 2\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_r\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_g\right)^{1/2}\right)$$

where:

  • $\boldsymbol{\mu}_r$ and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_r$ are the mean vector and covariance matrix of features extracted from natural metamorphic protein coupling maps using a pre-trained structural network.
  • $\boldsymbol{\mu}_g$ and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_g$ are the corresponding mean and covariance values calculated from the generated coupling maps.

Lower FCD scores indicate that the generated evolutionary coupling patterns closely match the statistical properties of natural metamorphic protein landscapes.

De Novo Design Pipeline, Computational Validation, and Biophysical Characterization

Chapter 3: De Novo Design Pipeline, Computational Validation, and Biophysical Characterization

The de novo design of metamorphic proteins—polypeptides capable of reversibly or irreversibly reconfiguring their tertiary folds in response to environmental triggers—represents a fundamental shift in protein engineering. Historically, the "one sequence, one structure" paradigm of structural biology has guided design algorithms. Overcoming this paradigm requires a unified computational-to-experimental pipeline that can target multiple, distinct energy minima on a single conformational landscape.

This chapter details the operationalization of generative latent diffusion models trained on co-evolutionary couplings to sample bistable sequences. We present the mathematical and structural frameworks required to invert latent co-evolutionary representations into physical amino acid sequences, validate these designs in silico using state-of-the-art neural networks and all-atom molecular dynamics, and structurally verify fold-switching behaviors using biophysical techniques.

1. The End-to-End De Novo Pipeline Architecture

The metamorphic design pipeline bridges generative probabilistic modeling with rigorous physical and biochemical validation. Unlike standard single-state design pipelines (e.g., fixed-backbone ProteinMPNN), the metamorphic pipeline must optimize sequence space for compatibility with two distinct structural topologies, denoted as State $\mathsf{A}$ (the ground-state fold) and State $\mathsf{B}$ (the alternative metamorphic fold).

Stage I: Latent Diffusion Sampling

The pipeline begins by querying the generative latent diffusion model (described in Chapter 2). The diffusion model operates within the latent space of co-evolutionary coupling profiles ($J_{ij}$ matrices). By conditioning the reverse diffusion process on target structural features of both State $\mathsf{A}$ and State $\mathsf{B}$, the model samples latent representations that contain overlapping co-evolutionary signals. These signals represent pair-wise residue constraints compatible with both topologies.

Stage II: Sequence Inversion and Dual-State Optimization

The generated latent matrices are decoded to yield physical sequence candidates. This is formulated as a multi-objective optimization problem. We maximize the sequence probability under both structural profiles while selectively introducing localized conformer instability (structural frustration) to lower the activation energy barrier ($\Delta G^\ddagger$) between the two states.

Stage III: In Silico Validation

Candidate sequences are filtered using specialized configurations of AlphaFold2 (AF2) and RoseTTAFold. By manipulating the Multiple Sequence Alignments (MSAs) and template inputs, we force these networks to predict the probability of both conformations. Designs exhibiting high-confidence predictions for both topologies under varying input parameters are advanced to all-atom enhanced-sampling molecular dynamics (MD) simulations to map the transition path and estimate the free energy landscape.

Stage IV: Biophysical Characterization

Synthesized genes are expressed in a high-throughput format. Circular Dichroism (CD) spectroscopy is used to verify folding and monitor global structural transitions. Finally, Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) spectroscopy provides residue-level verification of the bistable conformational equilibrium, establishing both the structural fidelity of both states and the kinetics of their interconversion.

2. Computational Seed Generation & Evolutionary Coupling Inversion

Mathematical Formulation of Dual-State Optimization

To decode the generated latent co-evolutionary landscapes into viable amino acid sequences, we define an objective function that balances the stability of both structural states. Let $\mathbf{s} = (s_1, s_2, \dots, s_L)$ be a sequence of length $L$ where each $s_i \in \mathcal{A}$ (the alphabet of 20 natural amino acids). Let $\mathbf{X}\mathsf{A}$ and $\mathbf{X}\mathsf{B}$ represent the 3D backbone coordinates of State $\mathsf{A}$ and State $\mathsf{B}$, respectively.

The optimization objective is to find a sequence $\mathbf{s}^*$ that minimizes the dual-state free-energy loss function:

$$\mathcal{L}{\text{design}}(\mathbf{s}) = w\mathsf{A} \mathcal{E}(\mathbf{s} \mid \mathbf{X}\mathsf{A}) + w\mathsf{B} \mathcal{E}(\mathbf{s} \mid \mathbf{X}\mathsf{B}) + \lambda \mathcal{D}{\text{JS}}\left(P(\mathbf{s} \mid \mathbf{J}{\text{gen}}) \parallel P(\mathbf{s} \mid \mathbf{J}{\text{nat}})\right) + \gamma \mathcal{H}(\mathbf{s})$$

Where:

  • $\mathcal{E}(\mathbf{s} \mid \mathbf{X})$ is the physical or statistical energy of the sequence $\mathbf{s}$ threaded onto a specific backbone conformation $\mathbf{X}$.
  • $w_\mathsf{A}$ and $w_\mathsf{B}$ are weight parameters dynamically adjusted to balance fold stability. For metamorphic switches, these are typically initialized near $0.5$ to prevent one state from kinetically trapping the sequence.
  • $\mathcal{D}{\text{JS}}$ is the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the sequence probability distribution conditioned on the generated latent co-evolutionary matrix $\mathbf{J}{\text{gen}}$ and the natural sequence background $\mathbf{J}_{\text{nat}}$, ensuring structural viability.
  • $\mathcal{H}(\mathbf{s}) = -\sum_{i=1}^L P(s_i) \log P(s_i)$ is an entropy regularization term that maintains sequence diversity and prevents collapse into low-complexity homopolymeric regions.
  • $\lambda$ and $\gamma$ are regularization hyperparameters.

The structural energy $\mathcal{E}(\mathbf{s} \mid \mathbf{X})$ is modeled using conditional sequence probability distributions parameterized by deep graph neural networks (GNNs), such as ProteinMPNN:

$$\mathcal{E}(\mathbf{s} \mid \mathbf{X}) = -\sum_{i=1}^L \log P_{\phi}(s_i \mid s_{<i}, \mathbf{X})$$

where $P_{\phi}$ is the likelihood of residue $s_i$ given the coordinates $\mathbf{X}$ and the preceding decoded sequence context $s_{<i}$.

Implementing Multi-State Conditioned Decoders

To perform sequence design on dual templates, we adapt the standard autoregressive decoding scheme of structural design models. Standard structural design models process a single adjacency matrix calculated from C$\alpha$-C$\alpha$ distances. Our multi-state decoder constructs a dual-graph representation $G = (V, E_\mathsf{A}, E_\mathsf{B})$, where $V$ represents the nodes (residues containing features from both $\mathbf{X}\mathsf{A}$ and $\mathbf{X}\mathsf{B}$), and $E_\mathsf{A}, E_\mathsf{B}$ represent the edge features for both topologies.

The message-passing update for node $i$ at layer $l+1$ is modified to aggregate structural inputs from both configurations:

$$\mathbf{h}i^{l+1} = \text{Update}\left( \mathbf{h}i^l, \sum{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} \text{Message}\mathsf{A}(\mathbf{h}j^l, \mathbf{e}{ij,\mathsf{A}}), \sum_{k \in \mathcal{N}(i)} \text{Message}_\mathsf{B}(\mathbf{h}k^l, \mathbf{e}{ik,\mathsf{B}}) \right)$$

where:

  • $\mathbf{h}_i^l$ is the hidden state of residue $i$ at layer $l$.
  • $\mathbf{e}{ij,\mathsf{A}}$ and $\mathbf{e}{ik,\mathsf{B}}$ are geometric edge encodings (distance, direction, and orientation matrices) derived from backbones $\mathbf{X}\mathsf{A}$ and $\mathbf{X}\mathsf{B}$.
  • $\mathcal{N}(i)$ is the union of spatial neighbors of residue $i$ in both conformations.

This dual-graph message-passing scheme forces the network to learn a sequence space that satisfies the stereochemical constraints of both target conformations.

3. In Silico Validation & Multi-State Prediction Protocols

Once a library of metamorphic candidates is generated, it must be filtered computationally. Standard structural predictors are highly biased toward single-state predictions. Consequently, we employ specialized validation protocols using AlphaFold2 multi-state sampling and enhanced-sampling Molecular Dynamics (MD).

AlphaFold2 Multi-State Predictions via MSA Manipulation

AlphaFold2 relies heavily on co-evolutionary information embedded in MSAs to resolve structural ambiguity. For a metamorphic sequence, a rich MSA containing homologous sequences that fold into State $\mathsf{A}$ will bias the network to predict State $\mathsf{A}$ exclusively, hiding its capability to fold into State $\mathsf{B}$.

To assess bistability in silico, we implement three modified prediction modes:

  1. Single-Sequence Mode (No MSA): We run AlphaFold2 with empty MSAs, setting the maximum number of recycles to 1, 3, and 12. Removing the evolutionary bias forces the structural module to rely entirely on its learned single-sequence structural prior. If the sequence is bistable, different seeds or recycle depths will often predict different conformations.
  2. Shallow MSA Subsampling: Instead of using the full MSA, we randomly sub-sample the MSA to a depth of $N_{\text{seq}} \in [2, 10, 50]$. This limits co-evolutionary signals, allowing the network to sample alternative structural states across different random seeds.
  3. Conformation-Specific Template Seeding: We explicitly supply PDB templates of either State $\mathsf{A}$ or State $\mathsf{B}$ along with the query sequence, setting template_all_atom_masks to force coordinate conditioning. A successful design must yield high predicted Local Distance Difference Difference Test (pLDDT) scores ($>80$) and low Root Mean Square Deviation (RMSD) to both templates when matching templates are provided.

The performance of each design is evaluated using a dual-state confidence metric:

$$\Gamma_{\text{bistable}} = \min(\text{pLDDT}\mathsf{A}, \text{pLDDT}\mathsf{B}) \times \left(1 - | \text{pLDDT}\mathsf{A} - \text{pLDDT}\mathsf{B} | / 100 \right)$$

Designs with $\Gamma_{\text{bistable}} \ge 75$ are prioritized for dynamic validation.

Molecular Dynamics (MD) Simulations & Enhanced Sampling

Standard nanosecond-scale equilibrium MD simulations cannot capture fold-switching transitions, which typically occur on millisecond-to-second timescales. We therefore use enhanced-sampling methodologies—specifically Well-Tempered Metadynamics and Replica Exchange Molecular Dynamics (REMD)—to map the free energy surface (FES) as a function of specific collective variables (CVs).

Collective Variable Definition

To monitor the transition between State $\mathsf{A}$ and State $\mathsf{B}$, we define two CVs based on the fraction of native contacts, $Q_\mathsf{A}$ and $Q_\mathsf{B}$. The fraction of native contacts $Q(\mathbf{X})$ for a conformation $\mathbf{X}$ relative to a reference state $\mathbf{X}_{\text{ref}}$ is calculated as:

$$Q(\mathbf{X}) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{(i,j) \in \mathcal{C}} \frac{1}{1 + \exp\left[\beta \left(r_{ij}(\mathbf{X}) - \lambda r_{ij}^0\right)\right]}$$

Where:

  • $\mathcal{C}$ is the set of native residue-residue pairs $(i,j)$ whose C$\alpha$-C$\alpha$ distance is less than $8.5 \text{ Å}$ in the reference state $\mathbf{X}_{\text{ref}}$ (excluding $|i-j| \le 3$).
  • $r_{ij}(\mathbf{X})$ is the distance between residues $i$ and $j$ in the instantaneous structure $\mathbf{X}$.
  • $r_{ij}^0$ is the native distance in the reference state.
  • $\beta = 5.0\text{ Å}^{-1}$ is a smoothing parameter, and $\lambda = 1.8$ accounts for thermal fluctuations.

Metadynamics Biasing Potential

In Well-Tempered Metadynamics, a history-dependent biasing potential $V(\mathbf{s}, t)$ is added along the defined CVs ($\mathbf{s} = [Q_\mathsf{A}, Q_\mathsf{B}]$) to discourage the system from revisiting explored regions of phase space:

$$V(\mathbf{s}, t) = \int_0^t \omega_0 \exp\left( -\frac{V(\mathbf{s}, t')}{k_{\text{B}} \Delta T} \right) \exp\left( -\sum_{d=1}^2 \frac{(s_d - s_d(t'))^2}{2\sigma_d^2} \right) dt'$$

Where:

  • $\omega_0$ is the initial deposition rate.
  • $\sigma_d$ is the resolution of Gaussian width for CV $d$.
  • $\Delta T$ is a parameter with the dimension of temperature, regulating the rate of convergence of the bias. The bias factor is defined as $\gamma = (T + \Delta T)/T$.

By running $1.5\ \mu\text{s}$ metadynamics simulations, we reconstruct the 2D Free Energy Surface:

$$G(\mathbf{s}) = -\frac{T + \Delta T}{\Delta T} V(\mathbf{s}, t \to \infty)$$

A sequence is classified as a validated metamorphic design if the FES reveals:

  1. Two distinct energy basins corresponding to ${Q_\mathsf{A} > 0.7, Q_\mathsf{B} < 0.3}$ and ${Q_\mathsf{A} < 0.3, Q_\mathsf{B} > 0.7}$.

4. Computational Validation and Filtering Pipeline

The following production-ready Python script automates the classification of metamorphic candidates. It processes a trajectory of a candidate design, computes the fraction of native contacts $Q_\mathsf{A}$ and $Q_\mathsf{B}$ using atomic coordinates, and determines whether the design meets the criteria for bistability.

"""
Metamorphic Design Filtering Pipeline
This module calculates the fraction of native contacts (Q_A and Q_B) across
an MD trajectory to classify fold-switching candidates.
"""

import numpy as np
import scipy.spatial.distance as spdist

class MetamorphicClassifier:
    def __init__(self, pdb_ref_a: str, pdb_ref_b: str, cutoff: float = 8.5):
        """
        Initialize the classifier with reference coordinates for State A and State B.
        Parameters:
            pdb_ref_a (str): Path to PDB of state A reference.
            pdb_ref_b (str): Path to PDB of state B reference.
            cutoff (float): Contact distance threshold in Angstroms.
        """
        self.cutoff = cutoff
        self.coords_a = self._extract_ca_coordinates(pdb_ref_a)
        self.coords_b = self._extract_ca_coordinates(pdb_ref_b)
        
        # Calculate reference contact maps
        self.contacts_a, self.dists_a = self._get_native_contacts(self.coords_a)
        self.contacts_b, self.dists_b = self._get_native_contacts(self.coords_b)

    def _extract_ca_coordinates(self, pdb_path: str) -> np.ndarray:
        """Extracts C-alpha coordinates from a PDB file."""
        coords = []
        with open(pdb_path, 'r') as file:
            for line in file:
                if line.startswith("ATOM") and line[12:16].strip() == "CA":
                    x = float(line[30:38].strip())
                    y = float(line[38:46].strip())
                    z = float(line[46:54].strip())
                    coords.append([x, y, z])
        if len(coords) == 0:
            raise ValueError(f"No C-alpha atoms found in PDB: {pdb_path}")
        return np.array(coords)

    def _get_native_contacts(self, coords: np.ndarray) -> tuple:
        """Identifies pairs within cutoff and stores their distances, excluding |i-j| <= 3."""
        num_atoms = len(coords)
        dist_matrix = spdist.cdist(coords, coords, 'euclidean')
        contacts = []
        distances = []
        for i in range(num_atoms):
            for j in range(i + 4, num_atoms):  # Sequence separation >= 4
                if dist_matrix[i, j] < self.cutoff:
                    contacts.append((i, j))
                    distances.append(dist_matrix[i, j])
        return np.array(contacts), np.array(distances)

    def calculate_q(self, target_coords: np.ndarray, state: str = 'A', beta: float = 5.0, lmbda: float = 1.8) -> float:
        """
        Calculates fraction of native contacts Q for a given coordinate set.
        Formula: Q(X) = 1/N * sum( 1 / ( 1 + exp(beta * (r_ij - lambda * r_ij_0)) ) )
        """
        if state == 'A':
            contacts = self.contacts_a
            ref_dists = self.dists_a
        elif state == 'B':
            contacts = self.contacts_b
            ref_dists = self.dists_b
        else:
            raise ValueError("State must be 'A' or 'B'")
            
        if len(contacts) == 0:
            return 0.0

        # Extract distances in target coordinates for native contact pairs
        target_dists = np.sqrt(np.sum((target_coords[contacts[:, 0]] - target_coords[contacts[:, 1]])**2, axis=1))
        
        # Sigmoid filter
        exponent = beta * (target_dists - lmbda * ref_dists)
        q_sum = np.sum(1.0 / (1.0 + np.exp(exponent)))
        return float(q_sum / len(contacts))

    def analyze_trajectory(self, trajectory_coords: np.ndarray) -> dict:
        """
        Analyzes a 3D numpy array of coordinates of shape (frames, residues, 3).
        Returns classification metrics.
        """
        num_frames = trajectory_coords.shape[0]
        q_a_vals = np.zeros(num_frames)
        q_b_vals = np.zeros(num_frames)

        for f in range(num_frames):
            q_a_vals[f] = self.calculate_q(trajectory_coords[f], state='A')
            q_b_vals[f] = self.calculate_q(trajectory_coords[f], state='B')

        # Evaluation criteria
        max_qa, max_qb = np.max(q_a_vals), np.max(q_b_vals)
        transition_detected = np.any((q_a_vals > 0.6) & (q_b_vals < 0.3)) and np.any((q_b_vals > 0.6) & (q_a_vals < 0.3))
        
        # Calculate overlap in populations
        bistable_ratio = np.sum((q_a_vals > 0.4) & (q_b_vals > 0.4)) / num_frames

        is_metamorphic = max_qa > 0.75 and max_qb > 0.75 and transition_detected

        return {
            "max_Q_A": max_qa,
            "max_Q_B": max_qb,
            "transition_detected": bool(transition_detected),
            "coexistence_ratio": bistable_ratio,
            "is_metamorphic_candidate": bool(is_metamorphic)
        }

Circular Dichroism (CD) Spectroscopy

CD spectroscopy serves as the first-line biophysical check, measuring the differential absorption of left- and right-circularly polarized light by chiral amide backbones.

  • Secondary Structure Characterization: Far-UV CD spectra (190–260 nm) are acquired at $25^\circ\text{C}$. A classical $\alpha$-helical state displays negative ellipticity minima at 222 nm and 208 nm, and a positive peak at 192 nm. An all-$\beta$-sheet state displays a single minimum at 218 nm and a maximum at 195 nm. Metamorphic transitions (e.g., from an $\alpha$-helical bundle to a $\beta$-barrel) are observed as clean isosbestic points in concentration- or temperature-dependent spectra.

  • Thermal and Chemical Denaturation: To map the stability of both folds, we perform thermal melts by monitoring ellipticity at 222 nm ($[\theta]{222}$) and 218 nm ($[\theta]{218}$) across a temperature gradient ($25^\circ\text{C}$ to $95^\circ\text{C}$).

    For a bistable metamorphic protein, we often observe complex, non-two-state unfolding curves. If fold $\mathsf{A}$ is kinetically stabilized and fold $\mathsf{B}$ is thermodynamically stable, the melting profile displays a biphasic transition:

$$\Psi(T) = f_\mathsf{A} \cdot \Psi_\mathsf{A}(T) + f_\mathsf{B} \cdot \Psi_\mathsf{B}(T) + f_{\text{unfolded}} \cdot \Psi_{\text{U}}(T)$$

Where $f_i$ corresponds to the fractional population of each state at temperature $T$.

Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) Spectroscopy

NMR spectroscopy is the most definitive tool for characterizing metamorphic proteins in solution, resolving both structural states and the kinetics of their interconversion at residue-level resolution.

1. $^{1}\text{H}$-$^{15}\text{N}$ HSQC (Heteronuclear Single Quantum Coherence)

Uniformly $^{15}\text{N}$-labeled protein samples are examined in aqueous buffer.

  • Bistability Signature: If the conformational exchange rate ($k_{\text{ex}} = k_{\mathsf{A} \to \mathsf{B}} + k_{\mathsf{B} \to \mathsf{A}}$) is slow on the chemical shift timescale ($\Delta \nu \gg k_{\text{ex}}$), the HSQC spectrum will exhibit two distinct sets of peaks for a single polypeptide sequence. The intensity ratio of these peak sets corresponds directly to the equilibrium constant:

    $$K_{\text{eq}} = \frac{[\mathsf{B}]}{[\mathsf{A}]} = \frac{I_\mathsf{B}}{I_\mathsf{A}}$$

  • If exchange is fast ($\Delta \nu \ll k_{\text{ex}}$), a single set of population-weighted average peaks is observed:

    $$\delta_{\text{obs}} = p_\mathsf{A} \delta_\mathsf{A} + p_\mathsf{B} \delta_\mathsf{B}$$

2. CPMG Relaxation Dispersion

To quantify exchange processes occurring on the microsecond-to-millisecond timescale ($100\ \mu\text{s}$ to $10\text{ ms}$), Carr-Purcell-Meiboom-Gill (CPMG) relaxation dispersion NMR is employed.

We measure the effective transverse relaxation rate ($R_{2,\text{eff}}$) as a function of the CPMG refocusing frequency ($\nu_{\text{CPMG}}$):

$$R_{2,\text{eff}}(\nu_{\text{CPMG}}) = R_2^0 + R_{\text{ex}}(\nu_{\text{CPMG}})$$

For a two-state exchange model, the exchange contribution is given by:

$$R_{\text{ex}}(\nu_{\text{CPMG}}) = k_{\text{ex}} - 2 \nu_{\text{CPMG}} \sinh^{-1} \left( \frac{\Psi}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{\sinh(\tau_{\text{CP}} \sqrt{\eta_+})}{\sqrt{\eta_+^2 + \Psi^2}} \right)$$

Where parameters are functions of population fractions ($p_\mathsf{A}, p_\mathsf{B}$), chemical shift differences ($\Delta \omega$), and the exchange rate ($k_{\text{ex}}$). Fitting $R_{2,\text{eff}}$ curves yields the exact thermodynamic population distribution and the activation energy barriers of the metamorphic transition.

3. CEST (Chemical Exchange Saturation Transfer)

When one of the metamorphic states is sparsely populated ($<5%$) and cannot be directly observed in an HSQC spectrum, CEST is used. By applying a weak radiofrequency field at various frequencies, saturation is transferred from the invisible state (State $\mathsf{B}$) to the visible state (State $\mathsf{A}$) via chemical exchange, mapping out the structural and kinetic properties of the transient state.

Functional Switching Assays

To verify that the metamorphic transition drives a functional shift, we design sequences where one state is functionally active (e.g., binding an external ligand or acting as an enzyme) and the alternative state is inactive.

  • Ligand-induced Conformational Shifting: We assess the shift in populations by monitoring the HSQC or CD spectra upon titrating a specific ligand. The binding free energy shifts the equilibrium towards the binding-competent state:

    $$\Delta G_{\text{apparent}} = \Delta G_{\mathsf{A} \to \mathsf{B}}^0 - R T \ln \left( 1 + \frac{[\text{Ligand}]}{K_{\text{d}}} \right)$$

  • Enzymatic Switch Activation: For designed metamorphic enzymes, the catalytic rate ($k_{\text{cat}}$) is measured as a function of environmental triggers (e.g., pH, temperature, or phosphorylation status). This confirms that structural reconfiguration controls the assembly and disassembly of the catalytic machinery.
